About my research

Chiraikaribetsu River Sampling Site – Photo Credit: Takayuki Shiraiwa

Dissertation Abstract:

To fully characterize the impacts of global change on freshwater resources, we need integrated measurements and models that reveal mechanisms by which ecosystems retain and lose essential nutrients across scales. Collecting data at the “whole-ecosystem” scale provides insight into multiple governing processes that determine water quality. Here I will apply the framework of the critical zone (Earth’s thin skin from bedrock to canopy) to evaluate how interactions between watershed characteristics, suburbanization, and shifting seasonality exert controls on the spatial and temporal variability surface water chemistry. I believe that exploring these relationships through the lens of solute-flow dynamics will offer important insights to holistic ecosystem understanding. My primary study site will be the Lamprey River Hydrological Observatory (LRHO), in New Hampshire, USA, where I will leverage robust long-term weekly data records and high-frequency sensor data. First, I will leverage the high-frequency sensor network established in the LRHO in 2013; I will explore how process- and concentration-discharge processes describe the hydrologic relationship of solute concentrations across the reach. Second, I will utilize the longstanding grab sample record in the LRHO to investigate the potential contribution of subsurface solute pools to nutrient loading across the watershed and what connections may exist between geochemical signatures. Third, I will conduct comparative analyses between the Lamprey River and the Bekanbeushi River (Hokkaido, Japan), two spatially distinct but ecologically similar watersheds experiencing changes in land-use land-cover, to understand solute input dynamics across mosaics of human impact. My findings will serve to better inform communities and other ecosystem managers as they navigate the challenges of sustainably utilizing water resources in the Anthropocene. Undertaking these efforts will allow me to develop extensive understanding of how ecosystem processes across scales influence the spatial and temporal variability of surface water chemistry and the implications therein.
